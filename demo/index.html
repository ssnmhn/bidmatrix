00 · Before anything else

Three traps in this one RFP — each one a disqualification or dead points.

A 92-page RFP is not a document; it is a rulebook with contradictions. Our extraction pass runs every requirement against every other requirement. Here is what that found in FN2024-17 — real conflicts a small vendor bidding alone at midnight would hit:

Is the experience minimum 5 years — or 10?

§1.7.3 (p.7) vs. §1.7.3.2 (p.7) vs. §3.5 (p.24)

Section 1.7.3 requires "a minimum of five (5) years." Its own sub-paragraph then requires "a minimum of ten (10) years," with at least five under the current business name. Section 3.5 says five again. Three different numbers for the same gate.

✓ Resolution: satisfy the strictest reading — demonstrate 10 years, 5 under the current name, 5 incorporated in Florida — and show each number explicitly so no evaluator has to do the math.

Three references — or four letters?

§3.5(4) (p.25) vs. Form 8 (p.46)

The scope section asks for "three professional references." Form 8, forty pages later, demands "A MINIMUM OF FOUR (4) REFERENCE LETTERS." A vendor who stops reading at §3.5 arrives one letter short — on a criterion worth 30 of 100 points.

✓ Resolution: four letters plus the completed reference list. (References carried as much weight as Qualifications in this RFP — see the scorecard below.)

Bond required — or waived?

§2.8 (p.16) vs. p.25 vs. Addendum 1, Q17/Q26/Q30

§2.8 spends a page requiring a 5% proposal bond — then waives it in a checked box at the end. Page 25 separately says no bond. Two different bidders burned Q&A submissions asking about it; the city had to clarify it twice in the addendum.

✓ Resolution: no bond. And note the meta-lesson — the addendum also disclosed the $130,000/yr budget (Q15) and the incumbent's $6,800/mo price (Q3). Vendors who skipped the addendum priced blind.

02 · Deliverable two

The annotated outline — built in the RFP's own order, weighted by points.

§1.7 prescribes the proposal's contents "in this order" — so that is the order, exactly. Evaluators score against a checklist in the RFP's sequence; structural creativity reads as noncompliance. Each tab is annotated with the requirements it discharges, the criterion it feeds, its hard format budget, and the one sentence the evaluator should be able to repeat after reading it.

Executive Summary — cover page, table of contents, letter of interest

Discharges A-08…A-12Feeds: Qualifications (30 pts) first impressionBudget: ≤3 pages, single-spaced, 12-pt ≈ 1,350 words

Cover page must carry six specific items (§1.7.1.1) including the Project Manager's full contact block. The letter must address ability, commitment, understanding of local conditions, and approach — four named topics, one paragraph each, answer-first.

Panel takeaway: "This firm has done exactly this work, at this scale, nearby — and named the person who'll run it."

Proposal Form Package — Forms 1 through 9, executed

Discharges F-01…F-16Feeds: responsiveness (all 100 points depend on surviving this)Budget: as issued — no substitutions

Two forms require notarization (Forms 1 and 3 — Form 3 sworn in the notary's presence). Form 1's addenda checkboxes must show Addendum 1. Corporate certification (2A) needs a board resolution, secretary attestation, and seal. This tab is where "non-responsive" happens — see what it did to one bidder in §04 below.

Panel takeaway: nothing. This tab is scored silently, pass/fail — which is exactly the point.

Proof of Experience

Discharges E-01…E-05, X-01Feeds: Experience (25 pts)Budget: lead with a one-page contract table

Open with a table: contract, government client, scope, size, years — because the strictest reading of this RFP's tangled experience clauses (10 yrs / 5 under current name / 5 in Florida) must be verifiable at a glance. The criterion says "related government experience" — municipal contracts outrank commercial ones here, so they go first regardless of size.

Panel takeaway: "10+ years, government buildings, South Florida — next question."

Previous Projects — three engagements, past five years

Discharges E-06Feeds: Experience (25) + Qualifications (30)Budget: one page per project

Each project mirrors this RFP's shape where truthful: multi-building, occupied municipal facilities, day-porter plus night crew, background-screened staff. The evaluator is silently asking "is this our contract with a different logo?" — answer that question, project by project.

Panel takeaway: "They're already doing this contract, three times over."

Proposer's Qualifications & Licenses

Discharges E-07, E-11, X-01Feeds: Qualifications (30 pts) — the heaviest criterionBudget: licenses as exhibits, claims as one table

Copies of every license/certification go here as exhibits (§2.5 requires copies with the proposal, not on request). The tie-breaker rule (§1.9.5) resolves ties by firm years then PM years — so both numbers appear in bold in this tab's first paragraph. Free insurance against a coin-flip loss.

Panel takeaway: "Licensed, screened, incorporated here — and the PM has done this for a decade."

Key Staff & Subcontractors — Form 9 plus resumes

Discharges F-14, F-15, E-07, E-08Feeds: Qualifications (30 pts) "…of the firm and personnel"Budget: one-page resumes, org chart

The criterion's own words include "personnel" — yet most small vendors submit an org chart with job titles and no humans. Named people with tenure and Level II screening status (§3.4 requires police-approved screening before start date) directly answer what the operator-evaluator worries about: who is actually inside my buildings at night?

Panel takeaway: "Real named crew, already screened, with a transition plan for day one."

References — the 30-point afterthought

Discharges F-12, F-13, X-02Feeds: References (30 pts) — tied for the heaviest criterionBudget: 4 letters + annotated list

References are worth as much as Qualifications and double Pricing in this RFP — and the published scores show evaluators actually spread points here (the winner took 30/30; bottom firms took 15–20). Four letters (Form 8's number, not §3.5's), each annotated with contract size, scope similarity, and a named, reachable contact.

Panel takeaway: "I could call any of these four people right now."

Insurance Certificates

Discharges C-01…C-08Feeds: responsivenessBudget: certs matching all eight sub-clauses

Eight separately failable clauses: three coverage lines with exact limits, insurer rating (A.M. Best A-X), additional-insured naming, primary & non-contributory wording, waiver of subrogation, 30-day notice. A COI that shows $1M CGL but omits the additional-insured endorsement fails two rows while looking complete.

Panel takeaway: silence again — pass/fail, survived.

Price Proposal — Form 7

Discharges P-01…P-03, F-11Feeds: Pricing (15 pts) — the lightest criterionBudget: the form, arithmetic triple-checked

Fifteen points of one hundred. The bid tab for this RFP shows five-year totals ranging from ~$450k to ~$2.6M against a disclosed $130k/yr budget — and at least one entry so garbled (a $9,240 figure against a ~$588k computed total) that unit-vs-total confusion is the obvious diagnosis. We recompute every extension; the numbers themselves are yours.

Panel takeaway: "The math checks, and the price is explicable."

Additional Information — the two-page closer

Discharges A-13Feeds: orals shortlist decision (+25 pts available)Budget: ≤2 pages, hard limit

In this RFP, interviews added up to 25 points and reordered the final ranking (see §04). The closer's job is to earn the interview invitation: the two or three genuinely differentiating facts — response-time guarantee, local depot, retention rate — stated plainly, no padding.

Panel takeaway: "Shortlist them — I want to meet this firm."
03 · Deliverable three

The red-team scorecard — scored the way the panel scores, before the panel does.

On a paid engagement we score your full draft three ways against the RFP's published rubric, then hand you a ranked fix list. Below: how the protocol works, demonstrated against this RFP's actual criteria — with a worked excerpt for a clearly fictional vendor.

Step one: the disqualification gate runs before any quality scoring

Because responsiveness failure costs 100% of the points, no matter how good the prose is. Excerpt from the FN2024-17 checklist:

PASS RULE
Deadline & portal: received via DemandStar before 10:00 AM EST Jan 23 — late proposals returned unopened, no exceptions (§1.6).
PASS RULE
Notarization: Forms 1 and 3 notarized; Form 3 sworn in the notary's presence; names typed below every signature (§1.6, Forms 1/3).
VENDOR ✎
Addenda acknowledgment: Form 1 checkbox for Addendum 1 checked. One addendum was issued; an empty checkbox row is a responsiveness defect.
VENDOR ✎
Eligibility, strictest reading: evidence on the page for 10 yrs service / 5 under current name / 5 incorporated in FL (X-01) — not just the 5 yrs §3.5 mentions.
PASS RULE
Reference count: four letters per Form 8 — not the three §3.5 suggests (X-02).
PASS RULE
No bond attached: waived per checked box §2.8, p.25, and Addendum Q17 (X-03) — attaching one anyway signals the RFP wasn't read.

Step two: three evaluators, not one

FN2024-17's three real evaluators scored the same proposals up to 20 points apart — one proposal took a 90, an 81, and a 56 from the same committee. That spread isn't noise; it's three different reading styles. So we simulate all three:

Persona 1

The checklist evaluator

The procurement officer. Scores mechanically against the rubric, finds every missing form, deducts for anything they have to hunt for. Beaten by structure and citations.

Persona 2

The operator

The department that lives with the contract — public works, IT, facilities. Distrusts vague staffing plans. Wants named people, response times, a transition plan. Beaten by specifics.

Persona 3

The skimmer

A committee member with twenty minutes. Reads first sentences, tables, and reference letters. If your value isn't visible at skim altitude, it does not exist. Beaten by answer-first writing.

When the three simulated scores diverge more than ~15% on a section, that section reads differently at different depths — almost always fixable with a summary table or a stronger opening sentence.

Step three: every deduction states its evidence, its cost, and its fix

Worked excerpt — fictional vendor "Palmetto Facility Group LLC" (a sample draft we wrote to demonstrate the protocol; no real vendor's material appears in any public asset):

−4 pts · References (30) · sub-question: contract similarity

Letters 2 and 3 are from commercial office clients; the criterion reads "references/good standing with previous clients" and this panel maps references against municipal scope. Two of four letters answering a different question.

Fix: swap in the county library and school-district contacts [VENDOR: confirm both will provide letters within 5 days] — cost: two emails.
−5 pts · Qualifications (30) · sub-question: personnel evidence

Draft names zero humans below the owner. The criterion's own words are "competency and technical expertise of the firm and personnel." The operator-persona scores this hardest: who is inside the police station at 9 PM?

Fix: Form 9 + three one-page resumes with screening status and tenure [VENDOR: confirm crew assignments] — cost: ~2 hours.
−3 pts · Experience (25) · sub-question: the government qualifier

Six paragraphs of experience; the word "municipal" first appears in paragraph five. The skimmer-persona never gets there. The criterion says "related government experience of similar scope and size."

Fix: open the tab with the government-contracts table; move commercial work below it — cost: 30 minutes, no new facts.

What we never do

We never write a fact we can't trace to your certified intake or your documents — gaps ship as highlighted [VENDOR: confirm] flags, never as guesses. We never touch your pricing strategy. We never submit anything — you review, you certify, you submit. We do the paperwork; you do the business.
